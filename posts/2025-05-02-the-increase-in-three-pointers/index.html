It’s raining threes in the NBA. How has the three-point revolution affected the rest of the game?

Basketball
NBA
ThreePointers
Author

Arden Louchheim

Published

May 2, 2025

Sports are ever-changing entities and basketball is no different. Whether it is baseball players changing their swings to favor a higher launch angle that produces more home runs, more pass heavy offenses in football, or golfers using power to hit balls farther than ever before, every sport has seen its fair share of changes. However, one of the most drastic of these revolutions has been the way that the three-ball has taken over the NBA. Over the last 15 seasons and even before that, players have been letting it fly beyond the arc more and more often. However, the overall amount of shots taken per game hasn’t changed drastically. So which shot is decreasing in order to make room for the three-point revolution? Using data from the 15 seasons in the NBA this blog will answer that question.

First let’s simply look at the increase of three-pointers over the last 15 seasons.

So the three-point shot has clearly increased. Every team in the league has followed the trend of the increase in some way or another. But a quick skim of league stats could have given you that conclusion. Today we want to figure out which shot has decreased in order to allow for the increase in threes. The closest shot you can get in the NBA to a three that isn’t actually one? A mid-range two. Maybe the players simply decided to take a few extra steps back and try to gain that extra point.

The adjusted R-squared value of our regression line being 0.9645 indicates the incredibly strong correlation between an increase in threes and a decrease in mid-range twos, and implies that the relationship between these two variables is likely not due to chance. So yes, we have confidently concluded that the increase in threes is correlated to a decrease in mid-range twos, however there are other types of shots in an NBA game. Have shots at the rim also been affected by this increase in action beyond the arc?

The top yellow chunk stays about the same size over the 15 seasons, showing that the percentage of shots taken at the rim has not been affected by the increase in threes. In comparison, as the dark blue section representing percentage of threes increases each season, it cuts into the size of the teal bar which represents percentage of mid-range twos. Clearly, the increase in three-pointers has caused a decrease in mid-range twos.

This brings up a secondary question. Every graphic shown in this blog has shown an upward trend in threes. When does this trend slow down? Surprisingly, the league has not yet reached a point where three-point shooting percentage has gone down as a result of the increase in the shot. The graphic below shows that averages have stayed remarkably stable despite the jump in shots beyond the arc.

It will be interesting to see in seasons to come if teams ever reach a point where shooting threes ever becomes detrimental. As of now, no team has found the limit. The other worry concerns if the amount of three-pointers could ever increase to a point that begins to hurt the game. Fans love the long ball now, but if it suddenly takes over the league to the degree that it is trending towards, will the game still be enjoyable to watch?
Only time will provide the answers to these questions. But one answer we did find today, was that the increase in threes caused the death (OK that’s an exaggeration… aggressive decrease) of the mid-range two.
